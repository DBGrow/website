Getting Started

Welcome to the DBGrow Docs!
Here you'll find instructions on how to get started and information about how our system works.

To get started with DBGrow, All you'll need is a valid email address, Github, or Google account.
Signup for DBGrow if you haven't already,
once you complete the registration flow you'll be directed to Create your first Database!
Creating A Database

Registered users can Create a Database. You can have as many databases as you'd like! You can provision instances in any of AWS's 14 Global regions.
Databases are separated into 3 Distinct types: Shared, Standalone, and Replica Set.

Shared Databases are 100% Free and include 1GB of free storage. Free databases are great for learning MongoDB and supporting small projects. Your database will be hosted on an EC2 Instance in the AWS region of your choice, tenancy is shared between your database and other DBGrow customers in the region.

All Standalone and Replica Set DB's support the follow RAM quantities:
0.5GB, 1GB, 2GB, 4GB, 8GB, 16GB, 32GB or 64GB.

Standalone Databases are hosted on a dedicated EC2 Instance. You can choose any AWS public region and instance size, up to 64GB RAM
Standalones are great for small projects that need dedicated resources.

Replica Set Databases are networks of 3-50 dedicated Mongodb nodes spread between one or more AWS Regions and Availability Zones.
Replica Sets are great for sized projects and businesses that need high availability.


Now Let's create a database!

On the first slide of the Create DB flow you'll be able to select your Database Type. Depending on which type is selected, The next slide will request additional info to provision your DB such as the target region(s), RAM, and member count.

For example, here is the Replica Set slide which allows you to configure your initial Replica Set.

Note: You can provision up to 7 nodes across 3 regions to start. You can add additional nodes any time

After completing the slide, you'll be prompted for a database name.
Database names must be unique within our system, and at least 3 characters long. If a database name is too short or unavailable you'll be blocked from proceeding to the summary slide until the conditions are met.
Let's proceed to the Summary slide where we'll review complete the provisioning.


Here you'll see the summary for your new DB including all applicable Pricing, Regions, RAM, and Member Count.

Click the Create Database button to confirm the details and start your deployment.

If provisioning a Standalone or Replica Set and you have no payment methods yet, you'll be prompted to add one before provisioning continues.

Once you begin provisioning you'll see a progress bar along with status updates about the provisioning process. As soon as it completes you'll be taken to your new DB's console page.

Note: Provisioning Replica Sets across multiple regions may take a minute or two to complete.

The Console

Welcome to the heart of DBGrow! In the top panel you can see the overall health of your deployment, including RAM, AWS placement, and get connection codes in several popular languages.
You can upgrade to a different Database Type, change RAM, or add nodes to your Replica Set at any time by pressing the obnoxiously large Green Button ❤

In the tabs below you can execute mongo commands, monitor your deployment metrics, manage snapshots and backup, and adjust settings.

The Backup Tab

On the Backup Tab you can manage automated and manual snapshots.
To take a new snapshot, click the plus button on the right hand side. A new snapshot entry will appear with the current timestamp and snapshot size.

Rolling Snapshots are snapshots that are taken manually by you, or automatically by us. DBGrow takes snapshots each hour if you do not do so manually, so you always have a fresh save point.
DBGrow keeps up to 75 rolling snapshots. Once this limit is reached, new snapshots cause the oldest snapshot to be deleted.

Saved Snapshots are snapshots that are manually saved by you in order to protect them from auto-deletion. Saved snapshots can be shared with your AWS account so you can export your database in moments, natively! This process is described below in Export Your Database

Using the buttons on the right side of each snapshot entry in Rolling Snapshots you may Save the snapshot, Restore the DB from the snapshot, or Delete the snapshot.
In the Saved Snapshots tab the save button for each entry is replaced with a Share button to allow access from your AWS account.


The Settings Tab

On the Settings Tab you can change White List settings, including quick adding your IP address to the White List.

Note: IP Whitelisting isn't available for Shared plans

By default, there are no IP access rules. By adding IP's to the whitelist, you are making a rule that allows machines with these public IP address to access your database. This is an important security measure, so we recommend you se this up as soon as possible with your new Standalone or Replica Set plan.

Removing all IP rules will re-allow access from any IP address.



Import A Database
At DBGrow you can easily import your preexisting database from an existing provider, or from localhost access.

Depending on your requirements, there are several options to consider. Small databases that can be given temporary access (via IP whitelist, etc) to your new DBGrow server might use db.copyDatabase(). If you'd prefer not to, or cant make your old database accessible to your new DBGrow instance (VPC, Localhost development, Whitelist, etc) and have a small database, using a combination of mongodump and mongorestore might be best. If you have a large database that already runs on AWS you can share an EBS snapshot with us and we'll import it onto DBGrow for free! We also offer free migration services for large non-AWS databases if you provide us with a copy of your database files.

Please contact support if you'd like us to help you get your database up and running on DBGrow!

Certain examples require you to have the MongoDB shell installed. You can find the latest release of MongoDB here. All following examples were conducted with Mongo Shell Version 3.4.2.


With db.copyDatabase()
If you're willing to temporarily make your old deployment publicly available to your Standalone or Replica Set and have a small database (<20GB), copyDatabase may be a good option.

Before beginning please ensure your deployment has allowed access on the necessary ports for your DBGrow instance's IP. You can find your instances' public IP(s) address by hovering over each status circle in the upper half of your DBGrow console.

We recommend using the newer db.copyDatabase() method over the older copydb command, as copydb doesn't allow authentication via SCRAM-SHA-1 and has a few other caveats. If you're trying to transfer FROM a DBGrow instance to another host, you must use db.copyDatabase() as we use SCRAM-SHA-1 as our auth method.

In Linux or Windows terminal connect to your DBGrow instance: 


    mongo --host [yourdbname]0.dbgrow.com/[yourdbname] --username read_write --password [yourpassword] --ssl --sslAllowInvalidHostnames --authenticationDatabase [yourdbname]
    use [yourdbname]
    db.copyDatabase([old db name], [yourdbname], [old db address:port], [old db username], [old db pass], [mechanism])

You'll see {okay: 1} when the transfer is complete! you can use the db.currentOp() command from the shell to monitor the progress of your copy operation.


With MongoDump + MongoRestore
MongoDump and MongoRestore are a good option if you don't want to/can't allow access to your old database from your new DBGrow instance. The process involves taking a binary dump of your database using mongodump, and then using mongorestore to upload the binary dump of your database to your DBGrow instance.

In your Linux or Windows terminal on your old database server dump your database: 


    mongodump --db [old db name]

This will create a binary dump of your selected database in a folder called dump in the current working directory.

Once completed, use mongorestore to push your dump to your DBGrow instance. Make sure to add the correct path to your newly created dump as the last argument: 


    mongorestore --host [yourdbname]0.dbgrow.com/[yourdbname] --username read_write --password [yourpassword] --ssl --sslAllowInvalidHostnames --authenticationDatabase [yourdbname] [dump folder path]


From AWS Snapshot
If you have a large database (>20GB) You can provide us with a EBS snapshot shared from your AWS account and we'll take care of transferring your files over to your new DBGrow database for free! It's okay if your DB directory is located on the root volume or an external one. Please contact support if you're interested.

From DB Files
If you have a large database (>20GB) but don't use AWS for your hosting You can provide us with the zipped version of your DB files and we'll transfer them to your new DBGrow database for free! Please contact support if you're interested.


Export Your Database

You can use any standard MongoDB tool like mongodump to export your data, or receive an EBS snapshot of your current databases files. To export your database or a snapshot to your AWS account please view the backup tab in your DBGrow console page. Export a saved snapshot by pressing the share button.

Note: The snapshot shared with your AWS account does not actually copy the snapshot to your account, merely shares our copy with your account. To obtain a copy in your account you can create an EBS Volume from the shared snapshot, and then take a snapshot of the running volume. This snapshot will be 100% yours and will not be erased by DBGrow, even if you delete your original snapshot, database, or DBGrow account.
